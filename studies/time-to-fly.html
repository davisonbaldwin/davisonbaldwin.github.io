WORLD 05 · SONG + DANCE FILM

Time to Fly

The breakthrough of a man who realizes he can no longer let fear dictate his life.

Figures fig. 5a — dance film, full cut “Eight collaborators working toward one vision.”
The design question

How do you build out and incorporate the skill sets of many individuals into one professional product?

“Time to Fly” is a breakthrough staged twice — first as a song, then as movement. One person cannot make a dance film, so the real project was the team: a choreographer, two dancer leads, a videographer-editor, an assistant videographer, a costume designer, and a mix engineer, each with a craft I don't have, all building one product under my artistic direction.

Challenge

Assemble skill sets I don't possess — choreography, videography, costume — and integrate them into a single professional product without flattening what each craftsperson does best.

Decisions

State the idea clearly and early — fear, then flight — cast each role deliberately, and give every collaborator room to solve their part of it. The song set the arc, and the team brought it to life.

Result

A professional dance film that none of us could have made alone. Clear intent, stated early, is what let each expert do their best work.

The film puts the breakthrough into bodies. Donathan Arnold's choreography carries the arc I wrote in the song — hesitation, then commitment — through two dancer leads, Kiara Lee and Robert Berry, shot in open ground where movement reads as exactly what the song wants: room to fly. Hannah Wyatt framed and cut it; Caifeng Hong's costume design gave it a visual identity I couldn't have specified alone; Aviv Spitz covered second camera.

My part on set was direction, and direction here meant restraint: I knew the arc, they knew their crafts. The finished film is the version of the song none of us could have made alone — which is what the case study is actually about.
