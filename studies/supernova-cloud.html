CHAPTER 6 · INSTRUMENTAL

Supernova Cloud

An otherworldly composition exploring the universe of polytonality.

Figures fig. 6a · performance video “Composed in two keys at once.” “Made in the very moment it was played.”
The design question

What does music sound like when two keys share the same space?

“Supernova Cloud” is an otherworldly composition exploring polytonality, and it was composed in the very moment it was played. The piece is research in two directions at once: into two keys sharing one space, and into improvisational composition. The film was made with animator Caleb Worcester.

Challenge

Polytonality is usually heard as conflict, two keys competing. I wanted them to share the space instead, and to find that harmony live rather than on paper.

Decisions

Improvise the composition in the moment, and let neither key resolve the other. The piece stays suspended between them on purpose.

Result

An otherworldly instrumental: the same fascination that built the universe model, worked out in sound.

Polytonality is often written off as dissonance, two keys competing. Played patiently, it does something else: it makes space. Neither key resolves the other, so the ear stops waiting for a resolution and starts hearing the distance between them.

The improvisation is the deeper experiment. Composition usually means writing: sketching, revising, deciding at leisure. This was the opposite, treating music as an acquired language you can speak in. The piece was composed as it was played.

Caleb Worcester's animated film circles a ringed planet: a cold, pale body wrapped in a warm gold ring. Two things share one frame without blending into each other, the same idea as the harmony.
