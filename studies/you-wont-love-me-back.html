CHAPTER 4 · SONG + FILM · STRING TRIO

You won't love me back

A married man realizes his wife no longer loves him, and comes to accept that more than anything he wants her to be happy.

Figures fig. 4a · film, full cut “The strings say what the lyrics can't.”
The design question

How do you score the feeling a lyric can't say, and let your writing leave your own hands?

“You won't love me back” is a character piece: a married man realizes his wife no longer loves him, and comes to accept that he wants her to be happy more than he wants her back. The intention was to design that character's whole world in video and sound. His turn, the acceptance, is something the lyrics never say outright, so the music had to say it.

Challenge

The resolution, wanting her happiness more than his own, would be cheapened if it were sung outright. The music and the film had to carry it.

Decisions

Write the character first, then build his world twice: a string trio composed as its own layer and handed to real players, and a film with Hannah Wyatt that keeps the staging quiet.

Result

One designed world in sound and film, with the strings carrying the acceptance. It's the first time my writing left my own hands, and the arrangement is better for what Emilia and Lily brought to it.

Writing the trio was a different discipline from performing everything myself: the parts had to be complete on their own terms, because other musicians would be playing them.

The film Hannah Wyatt shot and cut is lit the way the song feels: one figure in a dark room, a single warm light from above. Nothing about the staging dramatizes the story. He sits with it, and the camera sits with him. This page borrows that lighting.
