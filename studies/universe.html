The design question

How do you make the largest possible information space feel navigable instead of infinite?

This project exists to teach. It is built for someone with no astronomy background who wants to understand the universe by moving through it, and its job is to make that complexity feel simple: real catalogs underneath (the HYG star catalog, JPL planetary ephemerides, IAU constellations, NASA imagery), one continuous interactive space on top. The central design problem is orientation: in an environment nine orders of magnitude deep, you need to know where you are, what you're looking at, and how to get home.

Problem

First-time visitors saw a beautiful void and left. The best feature, continuous zoom across six scales, was invisible, buried behind a heavy load and a wall of expert controls.

Approach

Design the first minute: a guided tour that rides the real camera outward, a minimap that answers “where am I” at every scale, curated presets in place of raw toggles.

Outcome

Time-to-interactive cut 5× with progressive texture loading; a first session now reaches the cosmic web instead of stalling on a loading bar.

THE AUDITThe problems, in the first minute

A first-time visitor landed in a beautiful, silent solar system with no hint that they could drag, scroll, click a star, or (the whole point) keep scrolling out until the Milky Way became a dot in the cosmic web. The single best feature was invisible. The controls panel read like a debug menu: thirty-five raw toggles, “Minimap” sitting next to “Alcubierre Warp.” And small things were just wrong: the minimap in sky view showed a cosmic-web map from a different scale, orbit view pinned “you are here” to the Sun at a distance of zero, and the search dropdown hung open over the sky after you'd looked away. They were small errors, but they made the model feel less trustworthy.

DECISION 01The tour rides the real camera

I designed the first minute deliberately. A welcome card offers two paths: take the guided tour, or explore alone (skippers get three hint chips that fade on their own). The tour rides the product's real camera through nine self-paced stops: the Solar System, the running clock, a satellite's seat, your own spaceship, then outward through the stellar neighborhood, the Milky Way, and the cosmic web, and home to tonight's sky, drifting slowly outward at each stop while you read. By the last stop you've already traveled the whole model once. A small ✦ Tour button keeps it replayable.

DECISION 02A minimap that's right at every scale

The “you are here” minimap only works if it is right at every scale. I rebuilt it: in orbit it tracks the actual camera (the old one tracked an unused flight camera, so “you” sat on the Sun); across the solar system, the stars, the galaxy, and the cosmic web it redraws its map to the scale you're in; and in the night-sky view, where a position map is meaningless because you're standing on Earth looking up, it becomes a view-direction compass: Earth at the center, a cone sweeping the sky as you turn.

DECISION 03Three presets instead of thirty-five toggles

The old panel had thirty-five raw toggles. Most visitors need three good defaults. I rebuilt the panel into collapsible groups (Sky & Stars, Solar System, Phenomena, Physics, Interface) under three one-tap presets: Essentials (the balanced view), Clean view (just the universe, labels off), and Everything. Change any single toggle and the preset highlight turns off. Every choice persists between visits, so the model opens the way you left it.

DECISION 04Load only what the first minute needs

The heaviest UX problem was the loading bar. The scene gated on tens of megabytes of 8K planet textures a first-time visitor couldn't yet see the value of. The fix is progressive: the loader now waits only for lightweight 2K stand-ins, then the full-resolution maps stream in one at a time behind the already-live scene and swap in invisibly. Time-to-interactive dropped roughly five-fold, and nothing pops or flashes. The first Jupiter you see is a slightly softer version of the one you can orbit a minute later.

DECISION 05The same universe, one hand

Half of first visits arrive on a phone, so the phone version had to carry the full universe. I mapped touch onto the physics the model already had. Pinch feeds the same continuous zoom as the scroll wheel, so two fingers carry you from Saturn's rings out to the cosmic web. Two-finger drag pans, a tap selects, and a double-tap flies you to whatever you tapped. One wrinkle: a pinch always ends with a finger lifting, so the app has to tell “selected a star” apart from “finished zooming.”

Flight was the one part I had to redesign. The desktop ship is flown with a keyboard. On a phone, a 🚀 button appears instead, and the ship starts at rest, because motion you didn't ask for is disorienting. Holding a finger down is thrust, and lifting it recenters the flight stick. The keyboard legend becomes a three-button HUD: Cruise, View, Exit. The guided tour adapts too. On a phone it teaches the 🚀 button, says “tap” and “pinch,” and info cards become full-width bottom sheets that slide over the sky instead of floating panels beside it.

The rest of the mobile pass chased each bug to its cause. The minimap's scale ladder overflowed its card on actual phones: enlarging the touch targets with horizontal padding had quietly made the row wider than its container, so the touch chips now grow vertically only. Render resolution is capped lower on phones to save battery and heat. The progressive 2K textures matter twice as much over cellular. Form inputs hold 16px so iOS stops zooming the page when you tap them, the layout respects the notch, the star twinkle turns itself off for people who prefer reduced motion, and if WebGL isn't available the loader says so instead of spinning forever.

DECISION 06The camera never moves uninvited

Clicking a star used to zoom toward it. It took three versions to learn why that felt wrong. The first version recentered the camera on the clicked star, and the view lurched. The second replaced the lurch with a smooth glide, and it still felt like the model had grabbed the wheel. The third version moves nothing: a click draws a ring around the star, opens its card, and quietly re-anchors the zoom axis, so the star eases toward center only as you choose to zoom in. Back out again and your original framing returns exactly.

The principle that fell out of those three rounds now governs the whole model: any camera motion the user didn't ask for is a bug. It shaped the fly-mode captures too. Early on, returning to the solar system meant hitting a pinpoint target in three dimensions, and most approaches missed. Now aiming anywhere near the Sun inside an approach cone carries you in, with a halo of rings growing ahead as the capture takes hold, so arrival reads as something you did on purpose.

THE CUTSWhat I built and then deleted

Some of the most useful design work on this model was deletion. Detailed 3D asteroid models, procedurally cratered and lit, replaced the simple glowing markers and made the belt read as clutter; the markers went back in. Scale models of the Apollo landers and Soviet rovers on the Moon looked like toys next to real imagery; the plain gold site markers said more. A rocket launcher with animated exhaust was fun for one flight and gimmicky by the third; deleted outright, about 235 lines. And a full set of speculative megastructures (a Dyson sphere, a space elevator, an Alcubierre warp bubble) was cut on principle: everything else in the model is real, and fiction sitting next to catalog data undermines the catalog data.

Each cut followed the same test. If a feature makes the model more impressive but less credible, it goes. The physics visualizations stayed (spacetime curvature, the dark-matter halo) because they illustrate real science; the props went because they didn't.

THE DETAILSGetting the small things right

The same principle runs under the surface: search closes when you click away and reopens where you left it; keyboard shortcuts survive the search box; the scale readout always tells you how wide the view is and how long light takes to cross it; star cards tell you when the light you're seeing left its star. The model also fails politely: if WebGL is unavailable the loader explains itself, the star twinkle turns off for visitors who prefer reduced motion, and every data source and artwork license ships as an attribution block inside the product. Nobody asks for these features, but they're what make the model feel dependable.
